For a production deployment, the next step would be to harden the crawler around durability, politeness, and operational visibility. The current single-node SQLite design is appropriate for a local exercise and small deployments, but in production I would move crawl frontier management and indexing into more explicitly durable components, such as a dedicated relational database or log-backed queue, and persist enough worker state to resume safely after interruption. I would also add robots.txt support, per-host rate limiting, retry policies with exponential backoff, content hashing for duplicate detection, and structured logging/metrics for crawl throughput, failures, and index freshness.

If search must remain available during active indexing at larger scale, I would keep the incremental indexing model but separate fetch/parse from query-serving more cleanly. A practical next step would be to continue using a single-machine architecture initially, while introducing a more robust storage engine for the inverted index and frontier, plus health checks and dashboards. If scale later exceeds one machine, the current separation between crawling, storage, and search makes it straightforward to evolve into a distributed design with partitioned frontier queues and a dedicated search backend.
